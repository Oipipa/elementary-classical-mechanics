Relativistic Kinematics

Lorentz Transformations

  • We solve for a linear change of coordinates $(x,y,t)\mapsto(x',y',t')$ that ensures both:
    1. Uniform relative motion along the $x$-axis with speed $v$.
    2. Invariance of the speed of light (so that a pulse traveling at $c$ in one frame still travels at $c$ in the other).
  • We suppose that $x'$ and $t'$ depend linearly on $x$ and $t$ (and trivially $y' = y$ if motion is only along $x$). Symbolically,

$$ \begin{cases} x' = A\,x \;+\; B\,t,\\ t' = C\,x \;+\; D\,t,\\ y' = y. \end{cases} $$

  • The constants $A, B, C, D$ must be found from physical requirements.
  • From the point of view of the $(x,y,t)$ system, the origin of $(x',y',t')$ moves along the $x$-axis with speed $v$. In other words, the event “$x' = 0$” must coincide with $x = v t$. That enforces one relationship between $A$ and $B$.
  • From the point of view of the primed system, the origin of $(x,y,t)$ is seen to move with speed $-v$ along the $x'$-axis. That sets up another equation that ties $C$ and $D$ to $A$ and $B$.
  • To respect the principle of relativity, a light pulse that moves at speed $c$ along the $x$-axis in one frame must also be measured to move at speed $c$ in the other. Technically, this is the condition

$$ x = c t \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad x' = c\,t'. $$

  • Because the transformations are linear, one can also look at a pulse traveling in the $y$ direction (or any other direction) and demand the same speed $c$. These conditions pin down $A,B,C,D$ so that

$$ A = \gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}, \quad B = -\gamma\,\frac{v}{c}, \quad C = -\gamma\,\frac{v}{c}, \quad D = \gamma. $$

  • Rewriting them in the usual form gives the standard Lorentz transformation equations:

$$ \begin{cases} x' = \gamma \bigl(x - v t\bigr),\\ t' = \gamma \bigl(t - \frac{v\,x}{c^2}\bigr),\\ y' = y. \end{cases} $$

  • $\gamma$ is often called the Lorentz factor, and it “stretches” time and length in a way that preserves the constancy of the speed of light.
  • The transformation shows how coordinates of events seen by one inertial observer (with axes $(x,y,t)$) are related to coordinates of the same events measured by another inertial observer moving at speed $v$ along $x$.

The Lorentz Contraction

  1. Setup
    • Consider a stick at rest in the primed coordinate system $(x', y', t')$.
    • The stick lies along the $x'$-axis, with endpoints at $x'_A$ and $x'_B$.

  • Its proper length (also called rest length) is
    $$ \ell_0 = x'_B - x'_A. $$

  • In the lab frame (the unprimed system $(x,y,t)$), the stick moves with speed $v$ along the $x$-axis.

  • Observing the Length in the Lab Frame

    • To measure the stick’s length $\ell$ in the lab frame, both endpoints must be measured at the same time in that frame.
    • Let that common measurement time in the lab frame be $t$.

  • Applying the Lorentz Transformation

    • The Lorentz transformation for motion along the $x$-axis is: $$ x' = \gamma \bigl( x - vt \bigr), \quad t' = \gamma \Bigl(t - \frac{v\,x}{c^2}\Bigr), $$ with $\displaystyle \gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}\,.$

  • Endpoints in the Lab Frame

    • In the stick’s rest frame, the endpoints are $x'_A$ and $x'_B$.
    • In the lab frame at time $t$, those points correspond to $x_A(t)$ and $x_B(t)$ such that $$ x'_A = \gamma \bigl( x_A - vt \bigr), \quad x'_B = \gamma \bigl( x_B - vt \bigr). $$

  • The Observed (Contracted) Length

    • The lab-frame length of the stick at time $t$ is $\ell = x_B - x_A$.
    • By subtracting the Lorentz-transformed coordinates, one finds: $$ \ell = \frac{\ell_0}{\gamma} \;=\;\ell_0 \sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}. $$
    • Hence, the length observed in the lab frame is shorter than the rest length $\ell_0$.

  • Physical Interpretation

    • This phenomenon is Lorentz contraction: an object in motion along your axis of observation appears contracted.
    • As $v \to c$, $\ell \to 0$ for the direction of motion.

Time Dilation

  1. Proper Time Interval

    • Consider a clock at rest in the $(x',y',t')$ frame.
    • Two events, $A$ and $B$, occur at the same spatial location $x'_0$ in that rest frame but at different times $t'_A$ and $t'_B$.
    • The time interval between the two events in the rest frame is
      $$ \tau = t'_B - t'_A. $$
    • $\tau$ is called the proper time for these events (because it is measured where they occur).

  2. Time Interval in the Lab Frame

    • In the lab frame $(x,y,t)$, the same two events occur at times $t_A$ and $t_B$.
    • We want to relate $T = t_B - t_A$ to the proper time $\tau$.

  3. Using the Lorentz Transformation

    • The time transformation is: $$ t = \gamma \Bigl( t' + \frac{v\,x'}{c^2}\Bigr). $$
    • Because $x'_0$ is fixed (same location for both events), $x'_A = x'_B = x'_0$.

  4. Deriving Time Dilation

    • Write down $t_A$ and $t_B$: $$ t_A = \gamma \Bigl(t'_A + \frac{v\,x'_0}{c^2}\Bigr), \quad t_B = \gamma \Bigl(t'_B + \frac{v\,x'_0}{c^2}\Bigr). $$
    • Subtract to find $T = t_B - t_A$: $$ T = \gamma \bigl( t'_B - t'_A \bigr) = \gamma\,\tau. $$
    • Thus, $$ T = \gamma \,\tau = \frac{\tau}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}. $$

  5. Physical Meaning

    • The time interval measured in the lab frame is larger than the proper time interval $\tau$.
    • This is time dilation: a moving clock (moving relative to the observer) “runs slower” than one at rest relative to the observer.
    • If $v$ is close to $c$, $\gamma$ becomes very large, and the moving clock appears significantly slowed down.

Doppler Shift

Doppler Shift for Sound in a Medium

  1. Basic Setup
    • Sound waves travel through a material medium (e.g., air) with speed $w$, determined by the properties of the medium.
    • The motion of the source or the observer relative to the medium affects the observed frequency.
  2. Moving Source (Classical)
    • Suppose the source moves toward a stationary observer at speed $v$ (along the line joining source and observer).
    • The source emits pulses at frequency $\nu_0$ (or period $\tau_0 = 1/\nu_0$).
    • Because the source advances toward each newly emitted pulse, the spacing between successive pulses shrinks in the medium.
    • The distance between pulses becomes $$ L = w \,\tau_0 - v \,\tau_0 = (w - v)\,\frac{1}{\nu_0}. $$
  3. Hence, the observer sees pulses arrive at a rate $$ \nu_D = \frac{w}{L} = \nu_0 \,\frac{w}{w-v} = \nu_0 \,\frac{1}{1 - (v/w)}. $$

  4. For $v>0$ (approaching), $\nu_D > \nu_0$; for $v<0$ (receding), $\nu_D < \nu_0$.

  5. Moving Observer (Classical)

    • If instead the source is at rest in the medium and the observer moves at speed $v$ toward the source:
    • The wavefronts still travel outward at speed $w$, but the observer intercepts them more rapidly.
    • The effective speed at which wavefronts strike the observer is $w + v$, and the separation between wave crests (in the medium) is $L = w \cdot \tau_0 = w/\nu_0$.
    • So $$ \nu_D = \frac{w + v}{L} = \nu_0 \,\frac{w + v}{w} = \nu_0 \,\Bigl(1 + \frac{v}{w}\Bigr). $$
  6. Notice that for sound, the formula differs between a moving source and a moving observer because there is a preferred frame: the rest frame of the medium.

Relativistic Doppler Effect (Light)

  1. No Preferred Frame

    • Light propagates in vacuum with speed $c$, independent of any observer’s motion.
    • Special relativity forbids singling out a “medium at rest,” so the shift cannot depend on who is “really moving.”
    • Only the relative velocity $v$ matters.

  2. Time Dilation

    • If a light source flashes at period $\tau_0$ in its own rest frame, then to an observer who sees the source moving at speed $v$, that period appears dilated: $$ \tau = \gamma \,\tau_0, \quad \gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \tfrac{v^2}{c^2}}}. $$
    • This affects how often flashes are received.

  3. Observed Frequency (Along Line of Motion)

    • The relativistic Doppler formula for light when the source moves directly toward or away from the observer is $$ \nu_D = \nu_0 \,\sqrt{\frac{1 + \tfrac{v}{c}}{1 - \tfrac{v}{c}}}. $$
    • Equivalently, $$ \nu_D = \nu_0 \,\frac{1}{1 - \tfrac{v}{c}} \quad (\text{approaching}), \quad \text{or} \quad \nu_D = \nu_0 \,\frac{1}{1 + \tfrac{v}{c}} \quad (\text{receding}), $$ in certain sign conventions. The most common compact form is $$ \nu_D = \nu_0 \,\sqrt{\frac{1 + \frac{v}{c}}{1 - \frac{v}{c}}}. $$

  4. Off-Axis Motion

    • If the source moves at an angle $\theta$ relative to the line from source to observer, one finds a more general formula: $$ \nu_D = \nu_0 \,\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}} \,\Big/\bigl[1 - \tfrac{v}{c}\cos \theta\bigr]. $$
    • Here, $\theta$ is the angle measured in the observer’s rest frame.
    • For $\theta = 0$ (directly along the line of motion), this reduces to the earlier 1D result.

Relativistic Momentum and Energy

Momentum

  • Consider a glancing elastic collision between two identical particles, $A$ and $B$.
  • View the collision in two special frames: $A$'s frame, the frame moving along the $x$ axis with $A$, and $B$'s frame, the frame moving along the $x$ axis with $B$.

  • Each particle has the same $y$ speed $u_o$ in its own frame before the collision.
  • The relative $x$ velocity of the frames is $V$ and by the law of transformation of velocities, the $y$ velocity of the opposite particle in each frame is $u_o/\gamma = u_o \sqrt{1-V^2/c^2}$.
  • Find a conserved quantity analogous to classical momentum. Suppose the momentum of an object moving with velocity $\textbf{w}$ is, $$\textbf{p}=m(w)\textbf{w}$$
  • The momentum is, in general, given by: $$\textbf{p}=\frac{m_o \textbf{u}}{\sqrt{1-u^2/c^2}}=m\textbf{u}$$ $$m=\frac{m_o}{\sqrt{1-u^2/c^2}}$$

Energy

$$K_b-K_a=\int_a^b \frac{d\textbf{p}}{dt} \cdot d\textbf{r} = \int_a^b \frac{d}{dt}\left[\frac{m_o \textbf{u}}{\sqrt{1-u^2/c^2}}\right] \cdot \textbf{u} \cdot dt= \int_a^b \textbf{u} \cdot d\left[\frac{m_o \textbf{u}}{\sqrt{1-u^2/c^2}}\right]$$ $$K = \frac{m_0 u^2}{\sqrt{1-u^2/c^2}}+m_0c^2 \sqrt{1-\frac{u^2}{c^2}}-m_0c^2=\frac{m_0c^2}{\sqrt{1-u^2/c^2}}-m_0c^2$$
