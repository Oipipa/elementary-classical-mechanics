No Friction

Problem Setup

  • The prototype oscillator is a mass $m$ acted on by a spring force $F_{spring}=-kx$ where $x$ is the displacement from equilibrium.

  • The equation for motion is hence: $$m\ddot{x}+kx=0$$
  • The solution is: $$x=B\sin{\omega_o t}+C\cos{\omega_o t}$$ where $$\omega_o=\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}$$
  • This is the natural frequency of the oscillator. $$\cos{(\alpha+\beta)}=\cos{\alpha}\cos{\beta}-\sin{\alpha}\sin{\beta}$$ $$A\cos{(\omega_o t + \phi)}=A\cos{\omega_o t}\cos{\phi}-A\sin{\omega_o t}\sin{\phi} = B \sin{\omega_o t} + C\cos{\omega_o t}$$
  • The standard form of the solution is therefore: $$x=A\cos{(\omega_o t + \phi)}$$

Energy Considerations

  • If we take the potential energy to be $0$ at $x=0$, we have $$U=\frac{1}{2}kx^2=\frac{1}{2}kA^2\cos^2(\omega_o t + \phi)$$
  • The kinetic energy is: $$K=\frac{1}{2}m\omega_o^2 A^2 \sin^2{(\omega_o t + \phi)}$$
  • The total energy is $$E= \frac{1}{2}kA^2[\cos^2{(\omega_o t + \phi)}+\sin^2{(\omega_o t + \phi)}]= \frac{1}{2}kA^2$$
  • Therefore, the total energy remains constant.

Time Average Values

  • The time average value $\langle f \rangle$ of a function $f(t)$ is given by: $$\langle f \rangle = \frac{1}{(t_2-t_1)}\int_{t_1}^{t_2} f(t) \cdot dt$$

Average Energy

$$\langle U \rangle = \frac{1}{2}kA^2 \langle \cos^2(\omega_o t + \phi) \rangle=\frac{1}{4}kA^2$$ $$\langle K \rangle = \frac{1}{4}m\omega_o^2 A^2 = \frac{1}{4}kA^2$$ $$\therefore \langle K \rangle = \langle U \rangle$$

Friction

Solutions

  • Discussion is restricted to viscous forces.

  • The friction force $f$ is therefore of the form: $$f=-bv$$ This is explained here.

  • The equation of motion is: $$\ddot{x}+\gamma \dot{x}+\omega_o^2 x = 0$$

  • The result is: $$x = Ae^{-(\gamma/2)t}\cos{(\omega_1 t + \phi)}$$
  • $A$ and $\phi$ again stand for arbitrary constants and: $$\omega_1 = \sqrt{\omega_o^2 - \frac{\gamma^2}{4}}$$

Complex Solutions

  • Cast the original differential equation to complex form by introducing the companion equation: $\ddot{y}+\gamma \dot{y} + \omega_o^2 y =0$.
  • Multiplying this equation by $i$ yields a new differential equation: $$\ddot{z} + \gamma \dot{y} + \omega_o^2 y = 0$$
  • Either real or imaginary part of $z$ would be an acceptable solution for the equation of motion.
  • Since the coefficients of $z$ are all constants, the natural choice for the solution is: $$z=z_0 e^{\alpha t}$$ where $z_0$ and $\alpha$ are independent of $t$. $$\alpha^2 z_0 e^{\alpha t} + \alpha \gamma z_0 e^{\alpha t} + \omega_o^2z_0e^{\alpha t} = 0$$ $$\alpha^2 + \alpha \gamma + \omega_o^2 = 0$$
  • The solution is: $$\alpha = - \frac{\gamma}{2} \pm \sqrt{\frac{\gamma^2}{4}-\omega_o^2}$$
  • Case 1: $\gamma^2 < 4\omega_o^2$ (light damping), $$\alpha=-\frac{\gamma}{2} \pm i\sqrt{\omega_o^2-\frac{\gamma^2}{4}}=-\frac{\gamma}{2} \pm i\omega_1$$ The solution is $$z=e^{-(\gamma/2)t}(z_1e^{i\omega_1t}+z_2 e^{-i\omega_1 t})$$ The real part $x$ is: $$x=Ae^{-(\gamma/2)t}\cos{(\omega_1 t + \phi)}$$
  • Case 2: $\gamma^2/4 > \omega_o^2$ (Heavy damping).
    In this case $\sqrt{\gamma^2/4 - \omega_o^2}$ is real and we get the solution for $\alpha$ $$\alpha=-\frac{\gamma}{2}\pm \frac{\gamma}{2}\sqrt{1-\frac{4\omega_o^2}{\gamma^2}}$$ Both roots are negative, giving us: $$x=Ae^{-|\alpha_1|t}+Be^{-|\alpha_2|t}$$ This is an overdamped motion showing no oscillatory behavior.
  • Case 3: $\gamma^2/4 = \omega_o^2$ (Critical Damping) $$\alpha = -\frac{\gamma}{2}$$ The corresponding solution is $$x=u(t)e^{-(\gamma/2)t}$$ $$u=a+bt$$ and the general solution is $$x=Ae^{-(\gamma/2)t}+Bte^{-(\gamma/2)t}$$

Forced Harmonic Oscillator

  • If the motion of the oscillating mass is opposed by a viscous retarding force and is driven by some external force: $$\ddot{x}+\gamma\dot{x}+\omega_o^2x = \frac{F_0}{m}\cos{\omega t}$$ $$x=A\cos{(\omega t + \phi)}$$ $$A= \frac{F_o}{m}\frac{1}{[(\omega_o^2-\omega^2)^2+(\omega \gamma)^2]^{0.5}}$$ $$\phi = \arctan\left(\frac{\gamma \omega}{\omega^2 - \omega_o^2}\right)$$

$$K(t) = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 A^2 \sin^2(\omega t + \phi)$$ $$U(t) = \frac{1}{2}kx^2=\frac{1}{2}kA^2\cos^2{(\omega t + \phi)}$$ $$\langle K \rangle = \frac{1}{4}m\omega^2 A^2$$ $$\langle U \rangle = \frac{1}{4}kA^2$$ $$\langle E \rangle = \frac{1}{4} A^2 (m\omega^2 + k)$$
