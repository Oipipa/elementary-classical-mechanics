The One-body Problem

  • Consider an isolated system consisting of two particles interacting under a central force $f(r)$. The masses of the particles are $m_1$ and $m_2$ and their position vectors are $\textbf{r}_1$ and $\textbf{r}_2$.

$$\textbf{r}=\textbf{r}_1-\textbf{r}_2 \Rightarrow r=|\textbf{r}|$$ $$m_1\ddot{\textbf{r}_1}=f(r)\hat{\textbf{r}}$$ $$m_2\ddot{\textbf{r}_2}=-f(r)\hat{\textbf{r}}$$ - The force is attractive for $f(r)<0$ and repulsive for $f(r)>0$. - Center of mass vector: $$\textbf{R}=\frac{m_1\textbf{r}_1+m_2\textbf{r}_2}{m_1+m_2}$$ - Since there's no external forces: $\ddot{\textbf{R}}=0$, which has the solution: $$\textbf{R}=\textbf{R}_o + \textbf{V}t$$ The constant vectors $\textbf{R}_o$ and $\textbf{V}$ depend on the choice of coordinate system/initial conditions. - Solving for $r_1$ and $r_2$, $$\textbf{r}_1=\textbf{R}+\left(\frac{m_2}{m_1+m_2}\right)\textbf{r}$$ $$\textbf{r}_2=\textbf{R}-\left(\frac{m_1}{m_1+m_2}\right)\textbf{r}$$

General Properties of Central Force Motion

Motion is confined to a plane

  • The reduced mass $\mu$ is used to turn a two-body problem into an equivalent one-body problem: $$\mu=\frac{m_1m_2}{m_1+m_2}$$
  • We can simply choose, without loss of generality, a polar coordinate system on the $xy$-plane to obtain the following pair of equations: $$\mu(\ddot{r}-r\dot{\theta})=f(r)$$ $$\mu(r\ddot{\theta}+2\dot{r}\dot{\theta})=0$$

Energy and Angular Momentum are constants

  • The angular momentum of $\mu$ has magnitude: $l=\mu r v_\theta = \mu r^2 \dot{\theta}$.
  • The total energy of $\mu$ is: $E=\frac{1}{2} \mu v^2 + U(r)= \frac{1}{2}(\dot{r}^2+r^2\dot{\theta}^2) +U(r)$
  • The potential energy is given by: $$U(r)-U(r_a)=-\int_{r_a}^rf(r) \cdot dr$$
  • We can obtain the following equation using $l=\mu r v_\theta$: $$E=\frac{1}{2}\mu \dot{r}^2+ \frac{1}{2}\frac{l^2}{\mu r^2} +U(r)$$
  • Introduce $U_{eff}(r) = \frac{1}{2} \frac{l^2}{\mu r^2} + U(r)$, the effective potential energy, so $$E=\frac{1}{2}\mu \dot{r}^2 + U_{eff}(r)$$
  • The formal solution for $r$ is given by, $$\frac{dr}{dt}=\sqrt{\frac{2}{\mu}(E-U_{eff})}$$
  • The formal solution for $\theta$ is given by, $$\frac{d\theta}{dt}=\frac{l}{\mu r^2} \Rightarrow \theta-\theta_o = \int_{t_o}^t \frac{l}{\mu r^2} \cdot dt$$
  • And $\frac{d\theta}{dr}$ can be obtained as: $$\frac{d\theta}{dr}=\frac{l}{\mu r^2} \frac{1}{\sqrt{(2/\mu) (E-U_{eff})}}$$

Planetary Motion

  • The potential function is given by: $$U(r)=-G\frac{Mm}{r} = -\frac{C}{r}$$
  • We can transform the equation for $\theta'(r)$ as: $$\theta-\theta_o=l\int \frac{1}{r(2\mu Er^2 + 2\mu Cr - l^2)^{0.5}} \cdot dr$$
  • The result is: $$\theta-\theta_o=\arcsin{\frac{\mu Cr - l^2}{r\sqrt{\mu^2 C^2 + 2\mu E l^2}}}$$
  • Solving for $r$: $$r=\frac{(l^2/\mu C)}{1-\sqrt{1+(2El^2 / \mu C^2)}\sin{(\theta - \theta_o)}}$$
  • Eccentricity of orbit: $$\epsilon = \sqrt{1+\frac{2El^2}{\mu C^2}}$$
  • Introducing the parameter $r_o = \frac{l^2}{\mu C}$, $$r=\frac{r_o}{1-\epsilon\cos{\theta}}$$ $$\sqrt{x^2+y^2}-\epsilon x = r_o$$
  • Alternatively, $$(1-\epsilon^2)x-2r_o \epsilon x + y^2 = r_o^2$$
